### C1  Retrieval practice beats rereading
- tier: core
- anchor: "practice testing and distributed practice were the only two high-utility techniques" — Dunlosky 2013
- model: Recalling is a memory-modifying event, not a readout — a successful retrieval strengthens and reorganizes the trace more than re-exposure does (the testing effect). Rereading only produces fluency: familiarity that feels like knowing without building the retrieval pathway you'll actually need under pressure.

### C2  Total spacing drives retention; the ladder's shape does not
- tier: core
- anchor: "absolute spacing enhances learning regardless of relative spacing" — Karpicke & Bauernschmidt 2011
- model: The causal variable is total time spread across reviews, plus a first retrieval delayed enough to take real effort while still succeeding. Whether intervals expand (1/3/7…) or stay equal barely matters — the meta-analysis (Latimier 2021) finds g≈0.03, essentially zero. So don't fetishize the ladder; protect the spacing and the effortful-but-successful recall.

### C3  Varied retrieval is what buys transfer
- tier: core
- anchor: "same-item recall d≈0.62 vs transfer to new questions d≈0.26" — Price 2025
- model: Drilling one fixed question trains recall of that question — you can ace the card and still fail a reworded version, which is exactly the interview failure. Asking the same concept from a fresh angle each time (derive it, apply it, break it) trains the generative understanding that transfers. This is why Whetstone regenerates the probe instead of storing it.
