### Q1
- prompt: MCQ — Of ten common study techniques reviewed by Dunlosky et al. (2013), how many were rated "high utility"? (a) five — including rereading (b) two — practice testing and distributed practice (c) four — including highlighting (d) one — summarization
- answer: (b). Only practice testing (active recall) and distributed practice (spacing) earned high-utility ratings; rereading, highlighting, and summarization all rated low.

### Q2
- prompt: Teach-back — explain why retrieval practice beats rereading, mechanistically. What is the act of recalling actually doing?
- answer: Retrieval is a memory-modifying event, not a readout: successfully recalling strengthens and reorganizes the memory trace more than re-exposure does (the testing effect). Rereading produces fluency — familiarity that masquerades as knowledge — without strengthening the retrieval pathway you'll actually need.

### Q3
- prompt: Why expanding intervals (1, 3, 7, 16, 35 days) instead of reviewing daily?
- answer: Reviewing just before you'd forget maximizes strengthening per repetition (desirable difficulty) and minimizes total time cost; daily review of known items wastes reps on easy retrievals that barely modify the trace.
